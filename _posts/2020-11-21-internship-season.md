Recently, I wrapped up my internship recruitment activities. I ended up accepting a return offer from Microsoft for Summer '21, which I'm extremely excited about! Recruitment can be a pretty stressful activity, so I wanted share my experiences and advice on how to approach it. Many things I write here are very specific to what I am familiar with. My experiences are from the perspective of an undergraduate Computer Science student looking for a summer internship in software engineering. If that's not you, I can't give you any guarantees.

#### 1. The Preliminaries

So, why should you read this, and who am I to tell you what to do?! Honestly, maybe you shouldn't listen to me! Everyone does recruitment slightly differently, and perhaps my style won't fit yours, who knows! I'm just a lowly undergraduate student after all. Regardless, I think it'll be good for you to get a feel for how other people recruit so you can get a wide variety of perspectives. My hope is that you can learn something from this and hopefully be on your way to getting yourself an internship.

But... to be fair, I'm not much of a writer. This post will heavily rely on me linking websites that contain good advice. I'll also be assuming you at least have a very basic understanding of how the recruitment process works, although I think you should be able to follow along even without knowing much. This post will also try its best to sequentially go through the recruitment steps, so it might not be best to skip around.


#### 2. Your Resume

As I said, my post will mostly be filled with links. Here's the first one: [This is What a _GOOD_ Resume Should Look Like](https://www.careercup.com/resume). My main takeaway from the page is that your resume should be as easy on the eye as possible. Recruiters spend a discouragingly small amount of time looking at your resume, so it's in your best interest to keep it as succinct and clean as you can make it. I also got a bit of advice about resumes from others, which isn't covered in the above link: 

- **Education**: If you are a student, you want to put your education first. Why? It's an easy checkmark for your recruiters. As a college student, you're mostly a product of your education, and showing it first gives your recruiters some reassurance. Most other students will do this as well, so it's in your best interest to follow the trend. 
- **Languages and Technologies**: You should have this as the second item on your resume because it as allows your recruiters to check-off another box. Recruiters generally do not have a technical background, which means it might be harder for you to impress them with your projects. On the other hand, the Languages and Technologies section is a straightforward way to show off your skills and knowledge about the latest and hottest technologies. It's probably one of the easiest ways to impress a recruiter. 
- **Projects**: One of the biggest concerns I see from someone looking for an internship is that they're either 1.) afraid their projects aren't impressive enough, or 2.) don't have any to showcase. I sympathize with these worries, since I also didn't have any projects in my first year. In reality, however, recruiters don't really care what you put. As long as you can demonstrate growth, knowledge, and/or desire, every project probably looks the same to them. Have a project that only took you one or two days? Put it on your resume! Have a class assignment that's literally only one file? Did basically every other student complete the same assignment? Put it on your resume! As long as you're honest about the work you did, you'll be fine. Recruiters are probably not the best at differentiating between good and bad projects anyways.

Some more ramblings: Your resume should also _exude_ confidence. Be proud of the work you've done, and be confident in it! When a recruiter selects a resume, they're essentially giving their endorsement that they believe this candidate (you!!) has the knowledge and skillset necessary to become an asset at company X. If you're not confident in your own resume, your recruiter won't be either.

If you're applying to summer internships, be sure you have a polished version of your resume by August at the latest. Most companies will start recruiting during August (even earlier if you look at fintech/banking/quant companies) so you want to be prepared when most positions open up. It's especially crucial you apply often and early, as you'll have a higher chance of being selected for interviews. One more thing to note is that you probably don't need to edit your resume based on any specific position. Most companies understand that students aren't specialized enough to have niche skillsets, so they'll be satisfied with a general resume. However, if there's a niche role (ex. robotics engineer) that requires some extra knowledge, you should focus on appealing your specific skillset. 

#### 3. Applying

So now you have a bomb resume, and it's early August. What do you do? Well, for starters, you check out [r/csMajors](https://www.reddit.com/r/csMajors/). No, seriously. Turns out CS majors are huge gossips, and they just LOVE to talk about the latest internship job openings. Try to check out their internship specific thread, which usually looks like **Internship Megathread Summer 20XX**. This will give you the latest information on what stage of recruitment most tech companies are at and allows you to keep in touch with the opportunities that are available. If you followed my advice, you probably already found this, but also bookmark the GitHub repo they have. This is what the [Summer 2021 Internship GitHub Repo](https://github.com/Pitt-CSC/Summer2021-Internships) looks like. These repos have a pretty comprehensive list of summer internship openings, and it should be your go-to guide when looking for opportunities. It's also crowdsourced, which means CS majors are intentionally sharing job postings they found, thereby increasing competition and making it harder for themselves. Honestly, kind of touching, right? 

I don't have much advice beyond applying early and applying often, as recruitment is a numbers game. You just have to apply to a lot of places and pray that you get lucky with a few. To give you an idea, I applied to ~100 postings my junior year, and ~50 my senior year. Many apply to a lot more.

If you're a first or second-year student, you're in luck. Make sure you check out the freshmen/sophomore specific internship postings. For example, Google STEP, Microsoft Explore, Twitter University etc. A nice thing about these programs is that you won't be competing against more experienced students. Another is that you're most likely guaranteed a return offer if you do get into one of these programs. Hopefully [this](https://www.reddit.com/r/csMajors/comments/jczqp4/internships_similar_to_google_step_and_microsoft/) gets you started.

Earlier, I mentioned you may not want your resume to appeal to a niche skillset. However, if you do, you might also want to include a cover letter (or even directly reach out to a recruiter!). If you really want to work for a specific field, you should appeal as much as possible. Those positions might be hypercompetitive (ex: robotics positions are probably mostly gobbled up by PhDs), so you want to give yourself every chance possible. Otherwise, don't bother. Your recruiter probably won't read it anyways.

My experience with cold emailing is usually not very positive, but I've only cold-emailed once. The recruiter was actually very pleasant, and I really appreciated how nice she was. They didn't have any job openings that might suit me though. I've also reached out to three separate recruiters on LinkedIn. I haven't heard back from any of them. 

Also, don't try to pull any silly tricks. Specifically, don't apply to a job posting twice under two different emails. It's unethical, probably illegal, and just plain uncool. Note: This does not apply to multiple job openings that are similar (ex. Amazon Canada SWE vs. Amazon USA SWE).

And lastly, keep track of where you applied and when (One time, I applied to the same position twice lol). You should probably do this on Google Sheets or Microsoft Excel.

#### 4. Career Fairs

I have close to nothing to say about career fairs, as I've only had unpleasant experiences with them. It's crowded, you get sweaty, and I never had a recruiter reach out to me after talking to them. If you do plan on going, just be sure to print out a lot of resumes and to perfect your elevator pitch. Also, try to target some of the smaller companies. You have a pretty good chance of hearing back from large companies even if you apply online, but smaller companies definitely appreciate the in-person interest a lot more. To make the most of your time, talk to companies that have medium-sized lines. They're probably your best bet.

#### 5. Coding Practice

Originally, I planned on writing a lot more for coding practice since it's really crucial to get right. I realized [other](https://seanprashad.com/leetcode-patterns/) [people](https://bharathpbhat.github.io/2020/09/19/laid-off-now-what.html) give much better advice than I do. Like I said, I'm also not much of a writer.


Having a good resume is important (duh). Realistically, though, it's really only around 10% of the battle. Resumes are your entry ticket into the battleground that is the recruitment process. You can't start your process without one, but it only let's you stand on the same stage as everyone else. Honing your coding skills is what will help you stand out from the crowd. 

Coding practice is arguably the most essential part of your recruitment process, but also the most painful. When practicing, you should follow a strategy. [Here](https://yangshun.github.io/tech-interview-handbook/best-practice-questions/)'s a good example of what I follow. In general, you want to partition the practice problems into different categories. For example, start with `string manipulation`, then `arrays`, `data structures`, and so on. Another good tip is to commit yourself to dedicating 3-4 hours of practice every week. Consistent practice is always a better bet than short bursts of intense studying right before interviews.

If you ever get stuck on a question and can't solve it even after looking at the solution, try searching the question up on YouTube! [Back to Back SWE](https://www.youtube.com/channel/UCmJz2DV1a3yfgrR7GqRtUUA) is a really good source of videos explaining how to solve some of the toughest interview questions on LeetCode. The discussions section is also a good place to look for alternative solutions.

Lastly, try to have a good idea on how to answer some basic behavioral questions! [Here's](http://dev.fyicenter.com/Interview-Questions/Sixty-Four/Question_1_Tell_me_about_yourself_.html) a good place to browse for some of the basic questions you should be able to answer. A lot of people underestimate the importance of preparing for a behavioral interview. Get this right, and you're looking really good!! If you also have time, try to fill out a table like this:

#### 6. The Interview

At this point, there's really nothing left for you to do, except to crush that interview. If you prepared by doing all of the above, then you're probably in pretty good shape. Here are some tips for the interview off the top of my head:
- Don't try to cram the night before. I can only imagine it being a miserable and stressful time. If possible, relax.
- Before your interview, try to solve a few simple questions you definitely know you can solve, like [Two Sum](https://leetcode.com/problems/two-sum/). It'll get your juices going and boost your confidence. 
- Always repeat the question back to your interviewer. This serves two purposes. First, it tells your interviewer that you were actually listening. Second, it gives you the chance to make sure you didn't mishear anything. It would be a shame if you realized you were solving the wrong question halfway through your interview.
- Walk through your own test case before writing any code! This lets you familiarize yourself with the problem statement and might also help you catch some corner cases.
- Write out your pseudo code as you go. This is useful because, let's face it, interviewers space out during your explanations too. Writing everything out gives them the chance to redeem themselves without asking you to repeat yourself, which wastes time. It also lets you keep track of your thoughts.
- It's okay to ask the interviewer if you can take a few minutes to think in silence. When I don't know the solution right away, I ask my interviewer if I can take a few minutes to silently think about it. Even if you don't come up with the solution during those few minutes, you can explain your new thoughts, which will come across more clearly than if you were just rambling.
- If you realize a bug in your code but don't want to fix it right away, just leave a comment in your code. You might be focused on solving a different problem instead; the other bug can come later.

#### 7. SNAFUs

SNAFU stands for Situation Normal: All Fucked Up. I'm dedicating this section to all of my interview failures because everyone has them, but I don't see enough people sharing their failures. I like SNAFU because you have to act like everything is normal during your interview, even though you know you messed up. I have a lot more than I list here, but these are the most egregious. Here are my SNAFUs.

- During my interview with Capital One (C1), I accidentally called them Bank of America (BofA). Honestly, it was an innocent mistake. C1 has a unique interview process, and they have three interviews: a technical, a behavioral, and a case interview. During the case interview, they asked me what benefits C1 had from acquiring another company. I was trying to think of real-life examples of banks acquiring other companies, and BofA came to mind. As expected, I didn't get an offer. They still paid for my round trip to and from their headquarters in Virginia though, so I'm content. 
- I actually had another SNAFU with C1. Their 3 interviews were 45 minutes each, with a 15-minute break between the second and third one. During my break, I became friends with another interviewee, and we lost track of time. I think I was ~5 minutes late to my last interview.
- My Facebook interview started off really poorly. The interviewer asked me to introduce myself, and I started to give my usual elevator pitch on who I am. Halfway through my pitch, I accidentally said "outside of academia, I'm a teaching assistant and research assistant." What I really meant to say was that I was a TA/RA outside of my classes.
- Facebook SNAFU part 2: I was asked a question I recognized from a previous interview with a different company. I then proceeded to completely forget how to solve the question. It took me quite long to solve it, and I became super flustered. I screwed up the follow-up questions and failed the interview. 
- My Two Sigma interviewer asked me to write test cases during my interview. Instead of writing JUnit test cases like I probably should have, I just printed out the outputs of my method in main, and just checked my outputs manually.
- During my DeepMind interview, they asked me if I knew any machine learning. I panicked and said no, which is suicidal, especially since they are a research company and specialize in machine learning/reinforcement learning. I actually had some very basic understanding of ML, but I said no because I lacked the confidence to talk about it with authority.
- Not with any one company in particular: I've messed up time and space complexity so often, it's embarrassing. 

#### 8. Conclusion

Recruitment season is messy, stressful, and tiring. You're probably going to end up hating this rat race. You'll hate the the coding challenges even more (especially CodeSignal). I hope this post helps alleviate some of those negative feelings by providing some guidance. I also hope my SNAFUs gave you some reassurance that stupid mistakes happen, even to the best of us. I can't guarantee you'll find success at the end of your tunnel (a lot is luck-based), but I really encourage you to take advantage of the vast amount of resources online and keep on trying. Like my best friend tells me, "prepare for the worst and hope for the best." Good luck to you all!
